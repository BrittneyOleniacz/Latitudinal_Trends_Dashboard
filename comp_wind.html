Wind speed is the highest at the greater latitudes. These regions are mostly tundras and glacial deserts (i.e., Siberia) because of the lack of direct sun. So, there is a lack of interference and competing forces, and many sources of wind. The air is denser in colder areas and sinks to the Earth's surface, thus adding to the surface winds along an orthogonal plane. Additionally, the Earth's rotation creates wind currents forced laterally from the equator to the poles. With a smaller circumference at the poles, the centripetal forces make for more compact air cells, intensifying the wind speeds. There is a bias here, seeing as there aren't many places to visit below the -40 latitude, south of Australia, and the southernmost point of the South American continent because there are no colonized areas in Antarctica. If there were, this plot would extend to lower latitudes and show the similar poleward forced winds, as seen in the Northern Hemisphere. At roughly 35 degrees latitude, the wind speed spikes above 25 MpH; this is most likely due to the regional topography of that recorded datum.
