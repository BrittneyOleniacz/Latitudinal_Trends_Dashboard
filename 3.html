New Jersey Amber

Raritan amber is the most significant amber deposit in the United States (Penney & Jepsen 2014). The Raritan amber laterally extends from Cape Cod, Massachusetts, into Long Island and Staten Island, New York, through Central New Jersey, before finally jutting into Maryland (Hollick 1905). Unfortunately, only a few excavation sites are remaining after extensive land development in the area (Grimaldi & Nascimbene 2010; Nascimbene pers. comm. 2019). Stratigraphy

The amber from Sayreville, New Jersey, is Turonian in age (90-94 million years old) and occurs in the South Amboy Fire Clay Raritan Formation and the overlying Old Bridge Sand Members of the Magothy Formation (Christopher 1979; Grimaldi et al. 1989; Lambert et al. 1990; Owens et al. 1968). The overlying Old Bridge Sand Members consist of coarse ferruginous sandstone (Miller et al. 2004; Troost 1821). The South Amboy Fire Clay is composed of 11 meters of think unconsolidated, finely laminated clay (mostly kaolinite), interbedded with fine- to medium-grained sand and a variety coarse lignitic peats (Hollick 1905). The amber is found in situ at the boundary between these two units, but most of the amber is found intermixed with fossilized wood in the uppermost meter of the South Amboy Fire Clay (Figure 3; Christopher 1979; Owens et al. 1998). The amber is present in the overlying Old Bridge Sand Members as inclusions (Hollick, 1905; Owens et al. 1998). Amber collection requires digging massive pits (Grimaldi & Nascimbene, 2010).

Depositional Setting

The lithology of the Raritan Formation indicates that the depositional setting was an interdistributary delta fed by slow-flowing streams exposed to tidal effects and alternating between freshwater and brackish anoxic conditions (Figure 4; Grimaldi et al. 1989; Grimaldi & Nascimbene 2010; Miller et al. 2004). The relative geologic stability of the region, as evident by the lack of faulting and erosion, indicates the amber deposits are in situ with no reworking (Grimaldi & Nascimbene 2010).

Amber preservation is highly dependent on the depositional setting. Most amber deposits are correlated with ancient shorelines and anoxic oceanic events that provide a soft bed of organic material for the amber to settle in and protecting it from the harsh and destructive forces of weathering and erosion.
