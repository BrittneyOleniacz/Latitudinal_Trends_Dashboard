Some Notes & Observations:

The Northern Hemisphere exhibits observable trends and strong correlations; in contrast, the Southern Hemisphere reveals negligible trends. Of course, there may not be any weather trends in the Southern Hemisphere. Still, due to the strong trends seen in the Northern Hemisphere, this conclusion is unlikely, and the assumption would be the Southern Hemisphere would display strong trends, much like its counterpart.

A glaring bias becomes apparent when examing the data; there is a disproportionate distribution of data points, or cities, across the hemispheres. As seen in the code block below, there are 395 cities in the Northern Hemisphere and 172 in the Southern Hemisphere. The northern hemisphere has over double the number of cities than the Southern hemisphere! This observation presents the question: if the dataset included more cities from the Southern Hemisphere, would there be a stronger correlation between latitude and climate variables? However, it is essential to note that there is less habitable landmass below the equator than above, which may be the cause of this disparity.

When looking at the city distribution, unsurprisingly, the more expansive countries provide the most cities in the data. In the southern hemisphere, the country with the largest landmass is Australia. Out of the 567 cities recorded in this study, 31 are in Australia. Russia is the largest country, not only in the northern hemisphere but on Earth. Thus when it comes to the data used in this study, unsurprisingly, there are more cities in Russia than any other country (precisely, 78 out of the 567 cities).
